About Me

In my spare time, I am an avid reader and baker, a beginner surfer, in climates with sub-zero temperatures, also a figure skater.

In my previous work, I’ve taken advantage of neuroimaging modalities such as functional MRI and electroencephalography (EEG) to shed light on the neural underpinnings of speech comprehension (Humphries et al., Frontiers Neuro 2014), the effects of rumination on negative affect (Lewis et al., Emotion, 2015), empathic responding (Decety, Lewis, & Cowell, J Neurophys 2015; Decety, Meidenbauer, & Cowell, Dev Science 2018), moral decision making (Meidenbauer et al., Child Dev 2016), and the development of intergroup attitudes (Meidenbauer et al., Dev Science 2018).

More recently, I have begun using functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS), which is a portable neuroimaging modality that measures changes in cortical hemodynamics, similar to fMRI. One of the key benefits of fNIRS is its flexibility and portability as it can be used outside a standard laboratory setting. I recently condcuted a study to replicate the load-dependent prefrontal activation effect in fNIRS with a large sample and robust statistical analysis. By employing a multivariate analysis (behavioral partial least squares), I also found that the relationship between activity in the prefrontal cortex and task performance was dependent upon the difficulty of the task (Meidenbauer et al., NeuroImage, 2021).

Addtionally, neuroimaging researchers have demonstrated the use of scale-free neural dynamics (as indexed by the Hurst exponent) to measure cognitive effort in fMRI and EEG (Churchill, et al., Sci Rep, 2015; Kardan et al., NeuroImage 2020). I was able to validate the use of this scale-free measure in fNIRS which can be applied to continuously measuring cognitive effort in naturalistic settings (Letourneaux, Meidenbauer et al., ADS 2020; Zhuang, Meidenbauer et al., biorxiv, 2021).
